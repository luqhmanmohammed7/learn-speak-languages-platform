CHAPTER 01
The Alphabet & Sounds
الحروف والأصوات

Every journey into Arabic begins with its beautiful, flowing script. In this chapter you will learn all twenty-eight letters, how their shapes change depending on where they sit in a word, and how to produce sounds that don't exist in English.

  • Recognize and write all 28 Arabic letters
  • Identify isolated, initial, medial, and final letter forms
  • Pronounce Arabic's distinctive throat and emphatic sounds
  • Read your first simple two- and three-letter words
SPEAK ARABIC · FROM BEGINNER TO PRO  ·  Page 6 / 154
INTRODUCTION:Getting Started

Arabic is written from right to left, and almost every letter connects to its neighbors — much like English cursive, except every letter joins, all the time. There are no capital letters in Arabic. Instead, a letter's shape changes slightly depending on whether it opens a word, sits in the middle, closes it, or stands alone. Don't worry — the changes are small and become automatic with practice.

CHAPTER 02
Vowels & Diacritics
الحركات

Arabic letters are mostly consonants — the vowel sounds are added as small marks called ḥarakāt, floating above or below the letter. Learn these and you can sound out almost any word, even ones you've never seen before.

  • Recognize and produce the three short vowels: fatḥa, kasra, ḍamma
  • Understand sukūn (no vowel) and shaddah (doubled letter)
  • Read the three tanwīn endings and know what they signal
  • Sound out fully-vocalized words with confidence
SPEAK ARABIC · FROM BEGINNER TO PRO  ·  Page 14 / 154
INTRODUCTION:Getting Started

In books for beginners, children, and the Qur'ān, every letter carries a small mark showing its vowel. In everyday adult writing — newspapers, texts, street signs — these marks are usually left out, and readers rely on context and memorized spelling. We'll learn them here because they're the fastest way to learn correct pronunciation.

VOCABULARY:The Short Vowels (Ḩarākāt)

Each mark sits above or below a consonant — shown here on ب:

ArabicTransliterationEnglish / Use
بَfatḥashort “a” — a small diagonal stroke above the letter
بِkasrashort “i” — a small diagonal stroke below the letter
بُḍammashort “u” — a small curl above the letter
بْsukūnno vowel at all — a small circle above the letter
بّshaddahdoubles the consonant — a small “w” shape above the letter
Buddy TipYusuf: “If native speakers skip the vowel marks, why bother learning them?” Layla: Think of it like training wheels. Once you've sounded out enough vocalized words, your brain starts recognizing whole words by shape — just like you don't sound out “the” anymore in English. The marks disappear because you no longer need them.
GRAMMAR:Tanwīn — the Doubled Endings

Tanwīn doubles a short vowel mark, adding an “n” sound to the end of a word. It almost always signals that a noun is indefinite (“a book” rather than “the book”) — a preview of grammar you'll master in Level 2.

ArabicTransliterationEnglish / Use
بًا-anfatḥatān — pronounced “-an”
بٍ-inkasratān — pronounced “-in”
بٌ-unḍammatān — pronounced “-un”

GRAMMAR:Long Vowels

Arabic also has three long vowels, made by pairing a short vowel with one of three “weak” letters. Long vowels are held about twice as long as short ones.

ArabicTransliterationEnglish / Use
بَاbāfatḥa + alif (ا) = long “ā”
بِيbīkasra + yāʼ (ي) = long “ī”
بُوbūḍamma + wāw (و) = long “ū”

CHAPTER 03
Greetings & Introductions
التحيات والتعارف

With the alphabet and vowels in hand, it's time to actually speak. This chapter gives you everything you need for your first real conversation: greetings, polite phrases, and how to introduce yourself.

  • Greet someone and respond to a greeting appropriately
  • Introduce yourself and ask someone's name
  • Use please, thank you, sorry, and excuse me naturally
  • Ask and answer “how are you”, with correct gender agreement
SPEAK ARABIC · FROM BEGINNER TO PRO  ·  Page 20 / 154
INTRODUCTION:Getting Started

Arabic greetings carry real warmth and, often, religious meaning. The most common greeting, السَّلَام عَلَيْكُم (as-salāmu ʻalaykum), literally means “peace be upon you” and is used by Arabic speakers of every background. The traditional reply adds a blessing back: وَعَلَيْكُم السَّلَام (wa ʻalaykum as-salām), “and upon you peace.”

Derived Verb Forms (II–X)
أوزان الفعل

Welcome to Level 4. Every Arabic root can grow into up to ten related verbs, each with a predictable shift in meaning — intensive, causative, reflexive, reciprocal. This is the single most powerful pattern in advanced Arabic vocabulary building.

  • Recognize the ten verb forms (awzān) by their pattern
  • Understand the general meaning shift each form adds
  • Use common Form II, III, V, VIII, and X verbs
  • Guess the likely meaning of a new derived verb from its form
SPEAK ARABIC · FROM BEGINNER TO PRO  ·  Page 119 / 154
INTRODUCTION:Getting Started

Form I is the bare root (كَتَبَ, “to write”). Every other form adds a consistent prefix, infix, or doubling to that same three-letter root, and the meaning shifts in predictable ways — this is how Arabic builds huge vocabulary from a small set of roots.

FormExampleMeaning

V — تَفَعَّلَtaʻallamato learn (reflexive of II)
VI — تَفَاعَلَtakātabato write to each other (mutual)
VII — اِنْفَعَلَinkasarato be broken (passive/reflexive)
VIII — اِفْتَعَلَijtamaʻato gather / meet (reflexive)
X — اِسْتَفْعَلَistaʻmalato use (seeking/requesting)

Passive Voice
المبني للمجهول

News headlines, formal writing, and polite deflection all lean on the passive voice. Arabic builds it with a simple internal vowel shift — no extra word needed.

  • Form the passive of a past-tense verb by shifting internal vowels
  • Form the passive of a present-tense verb

  • Read and produce simple passive sentences
SPEAK ARABIC · FROM BEGINNER TO PRO  ·  Page 125 / 154
INTRODUCTION:Getting Started

Where English adds “was/were + past participle” (“the book was written”), Arabic simply changes the internal vowels of the verb: كَتَبَ (kataba, “he wrote”) becomes كُتِبَ (kutiba, “it was written”). No extra words — just a vowel shift.

If this, then that. Arabic has two distinct conditional worlds — real possibility and pure hypothesis — and this chapter shows you exactly when to use each one.
